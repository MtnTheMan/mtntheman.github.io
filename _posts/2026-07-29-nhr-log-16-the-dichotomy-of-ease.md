Hardwoods can be stumpers, and I think any honest discussion about northern hardwood forestry has to begin with that admission.

I have already circled around this problem from a few different angles in this series. In [NHR Log 10: There Shall be No Universal Manual for Northern Hardwood Governance](https://www.mtntheman.com/northern-hardwoods/research/travel/2026/07/15/nhr-log-10-there-shall-be-no-universal-manual-for-northern-hardwood-governance.html), I was thinking about how difficult it is to write one clean rulebook for a forest type that changes so much from place to place. In [NHR Log 12: Good Forestry Without 50 Pages](https://www.mtntheman.com/northern-hardwoods/research/travel/2026/07/20/nhr-log-12-good-forestry-without-50-pages.html), I was thinking about how much good forestry can live in field judgment, marking, and apprenticeship, even when the written plan is thin. This post sits somewhere between those two ideas.

The thought I kept returning to in New Brunswick was what I started calling the dichotomy of ease.

In many situations, softwood work is simply easier to make repeatable. It is easier to write a prescription that can be used again. It is easier to train people into production forestry where the stand is clearcut, regenerated, planted, tended, and moved into the next rotation. The seed is often available. The seedlings are available. The market may already know what to do with the wood. The contractors may already know how to operate in that setup. If you spend the first few years of your profession learning that softwood work is the normal work, the easier work, and the work the market rewards, then the path of least resistance starts to look less like a choice and more like gravity.

A northern hardwood system asks something different of people. It asks for familiarity, patience, and comfort with complexity. If forestry is like being a car mechanic, then working in a softwood system might be like knowing your way around the same familiar trucks year after year. You know the parts. You know the common failures. You know where to look when something sounds wrong. A hardwood stand can feel more like getting underneath the hood of an imported Audi where everything is in metric units and it gets hairy really fast. That does not mean the mechanic is bad. It means the machine is different, and skill is partly what someone has spent years learning to see.

Foresters can be extremely good at working in softwood systems. Foresters can be extremely good at working in hardwood systems. A lot of it comes down to familiarity. If the institutions, markets, contractors, nurseries, mills, and early-career training all point in one direction, then the profession gets better at that work. Meanwhile, the other set of skills can become less common, less rewarded, and harder to pass along.

That is the part of the dichotomy that feels like a training and culture problem.

New Brunswick is a good place to think about this because forestry carries so much of the provincial economy. The province's own forest products page describes the sector as contributing about $1.5 billion annually to provincial GDP, with roughly 9,700 direct jobs and 14,100 spin-off jobs tied to the forest sector (Government of New Brunswick 2026a). Crown timber access, mill requirements, forest management agreements, utilization standards, and royalty systems all sit inside that larger economic machine. The provincial long-term planning page also describes forest management plans that look up to 80 years ahead and are updated every five years. In its current goals, New Brunswick names a wood-supply objective of increasing softwood harvest while reducing hardwood harvest to sustainable levels (Government of New Brunswick 2026b).

So when I say there is a softwood gravity in places like New Brunswick, I am talking about incentives, infrastructure, and training all leaning the same way. I am trying to avoid pointing the finger at any one forester, operator, mill, community, or agency. There is nothing inherently wrong with choosing the easy route if you have hedged your bets. Many towns and communities rely heavily on forestry. People need work. Mills need fiber. Managers need prescriptions that can be carried out by the available operators, with the available markets, under the rules and budgets they actually have. Still, a choice can be rational in the short term and risky in the long run, and that long-run cost is easier to miss when the immediate arrangement works. If a hardwood stand on a good site is converted into a softwood production system, the site may still produce a lot of wood. It may produce good softwood. It may meet a market need and keep trucks moving and mills supplied. But hardwood characteristics are much easier to remove than to rebuild. It takes time to grow back the structure, seed sources, quality stems, shade dynamics, operator confidence, and local market familiarity that make northern hardwood management feel possible.

A good site will often want to move back toward hardwoods if we stop pushing it in another direction. If humans disappeared tomorrow, a lot of those places would begin the slow process of reverting. Sugar maple, beech, yellow birch, red maple, hemlock, and other hardwood and mixedwood species would begin finding their way back where site conditions allowed. But humans are still here, and so long as the more profitable and easier system keeps being the softwood system, that natural pull can be overridden again and again.

Herbicide use belongs in this conversation for the same reason. New Brunswick's silviculture page describes vegetation management and herbicides as tools used to free young conifers from competition during the early years of plantation establishment or natural regeneration, while also stating that less than 0.5 percent of the province's forests are treated annually (Government of New Brunswick 2026c). That number keeps the claim from drifting into caricature, but the logic is still worth noticing. If the public investment is in establishing conifer plantations, then the next investment is in protecting those conifers from competing vegetation. One decision lays the groundwork for the next.

After enough repetitions, the easy route starts reinforcing itself. Markets for hardwood products weaken. Mills that once processed hardwoods close, consolidate, or move away from those supply chains. Operators spend more of their careers in clearcut systems where the point is to level everything efficiently, then later we wonder why fewer people have the touch required for partial harvesting in a hardwood stand. It is easy to teach someone how to run a machine in a clearcut. It is much harder to teach them how to move carefully through a stand where every tree left behind is part of the prescription.

That difference in care is one of the things I do not want to lose. A field story has stayed with me: operators can develop a real affection for the woods. They spend crazy hours out there. They see trees at a level of repetition and intimacy that most people never will. Foresters told me stories about operators calling from inside the machine because they were staring at a beautiful tree marked for removal and did not want to cut it. They had an affinity for the woods. They liked old-growth characteristics. They could see the difference between a tree that needed to come down and one that felt like it deserved to stay.

That is sentiment, yes, but it is also knowledge. It is the operator caring enough about the woods to notice what the prescription is asking them to do.

A hardwood system requires leaving something behind. It requires seeing future value in a residual stand. It requires care for the trees that remain, the regeneration that might come next, and the damage that can occur when machinery moves through the wrong place at the wrong time. If a whole generation of operators is trained mostly in systems where the job is to remove everything and reset the stand, then we should not be surprised when careful partial harvesting becomes harder to staff. At that point, we are doing no better than the Once-ler, except with better equipment and a more complicated stumpage system.

The ecological risk is tied to the economic risk. The basic resilience literature is messier than a slogan, but a consistent theme is that diversity can spread risk across species, functions, and responses to disturbance. Holling's early work gave ecology much of its language for resilience as a system's capacity to absorb disturbance and keep functioning (Holling 1973). Later work on the "insurance hypothesis" argued that biodiversity can stabilize ecosystem functioning when species respond differently to environmental variation (Yachi and Loreau 1999). The diversity-stability debate has caveats all over it, but the warning still works here: simplified systems can be productive, and they can also put more eggs in fewer baskets (McCann 2000; Oliver et al. 2015). In forests specifically, tree diversity has been linked to stand resistance to several types of natural disturbance, although the strength and direction of that relationship depend on the disturbance, species, and setting (Jactel et al. 2017).

That is the part that worries me when I see two species everywhere I look.

If a region becomes too reliant on a narrow set of species, products, rotations, and markets, then one or two bad diseases or insect outbreaks can put the whole arrangement under pressure. Maybe there is no more pulp because the thing will not grow. Maybe an invasive problem arrives that planting cannot overcome. Maybe the market shifts. Maybe the risk is fire. Maybe it goes up in smoke as an economic metaphor, or maybe, in some places, literally.

I think about lodgepole pine in the Mountain West when I think about this. Lodgepole was valuable, familiar, and widespread enough that enormous areas ended up with a lot of similar host material. Then mountain pine beetle, a native species, moved through with extraordinary force under warmer and drier conditions, helped by climatic conditions that affected beetle survival, development, and host stress (Bentz et al. 2010). That example is not a perfect match for New Brunswick, and I do not want to pretend that every softwood system is waiting for the same story. I am using it as a caution, not as a one-to-one comparison. When a forestry economy builds itself around fewer species and fewer products, it can become very efficient right up until the whole thing shakes.

Hardwood systems are no magic shield against risk. Beech bark disease, emerald ash borer, maple regeneration problems, deer browse, climate stress, invasive plants, markets, and poor cutting can all make hardwood management difficult. I am biased toward northern hardwoods because I like their complexity and because I think they have a place and a legacy on the landscape. That bias is worth admitting. A hardwood stand is not automatically virtuous, and a softwood stand is not automatically a mistake. The question is whether ease has become too strong a compass.

There are good reasons managers choose the easier route. There are budgets, deadlines, mill contracts, available crews, training pipelines, market histories, and local expectations. There is also a difference between what makes the most sense right now and what makes sense over the long run. From an academic perspective, I think we need to call back the dogs a little when we talk about what people should be doing on the ground. It is cheap to point at a place and say that managers should be doing harder forestry. It is harder to build the markets, training, trust, and operator skill that make harder forestry feasible. So I do not want this post to land on scolding.

The answer is probably not to rebuild hardwood skills from nothing. In many places those skills are still present. They need to be reinforced, valued, and passed along before they become rare. They need markets that make them worth practicing. They need foresters who are comfortable with complexity, operators who are trusted to work carefully, and institutions that do not accidentally train everyone toward the simplest repeat prescription.

There are ways to nudge forests toward better northern hardwood outcomes across the range, especially where people are encouraged to take the easier route. That might mean protecting good hardwood sites from quiet conversion. It might mean keeping low-value hardwood markets alive enough that improvement cuts can happen. It might mean investing in operator training for partial harvesting. It might mean admitting that a production softwood system can be useful while still asking whether we are overusing that answer on sites that naturally want more complexity.

Ease has its place. Forestry already asks people to solve hard problems under real constraints, and any practical forestry needs some amount of repeatability. But ease cannot be the only compass. If the forest naturally wants to host hardwoods, and the culture has learned to keep nudging it toward softwoods because that is easier, then the long-term cost will eventually show up somewhere. It may show up in the soils, in the markets, in the loss of operator skill, in simplified stands, in weaker resilience, or in the quiet disappearance of the very forest conditions we say we are managing for.

The dichotomy of ease asks what we are training ourselves to become good at. If we keep choosing the easier system, we will get better and better at that system. If we want complex northern hardwoods to remain a real part of the working landscape, then we have to keep practicing the harder work before the hard work starts to look impossible.
