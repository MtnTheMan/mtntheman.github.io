No plan is technically still a plan.

That is one of my favorite concepts that I wanted to explore while I was on the road, because it gets at something that feels obvious once you say it out loud and still very easy to avoid in practice. No management is actually a management action. You may make a cognizant decision to not make decisions, and at that point you kind of cede the wheel. You let natural course take its course, and whatever variables you have set in motion will move forward.

That applies especially to forest planning, forest management, and forest ecology. Forests are constantly changing resources. Even if you lack a plan, the world will keep spinning. The world will keep turning, and things will change in a forest. Not having a plan does not mean there will be no outcomes.

If you have an unhealthy system and decide to do nothing, it will likely keep moving along whatever trajectory made it unhealthy in the first place. If you have a system in decent condition, or one with healthy inertia already built into it, doing very little may continue to produce characteristics you actually want. Maybe that means older forest structure. Maybe that means a certain kind of vertical diversity. Maybe that means holding onto a recreational or educational value that the local public understands and appreciates.

So the question is not whether leaving a forest alone is always good or always bad. The question is whether we are honest enough to call it a decision.

There is a difference between strategy and tactics that feels useful here. Strategy is the larger overarching plan for what you really want to get out of your woods. Tactics describe how exactly you are going to get there. You can engage in tactics without a strategy. You can cut firewood, remove hazard trees, treat an invasive patch, sell a few logs, or decide not to harvest for another decade, all without a formal plan that says what the stand is supposed to become.

That kind of informal decision-making is common, especially on family forest ownerships. Much of the northern hardwood resource is held in private land ownership, and family forest landowners make up a huge number of individual actors across that landscape. In the 2018 National Woodland Owner Survey summary tables for U.S. family forest ownerships with 10 or more acres, 11 percent of ownerships had a written management plan, 84 percent did not, and 5 percent did not know. In those same tables, 18 percent of ownerships had received forest-management advice in the previous five years, while 82 percent had not (Butler et al. 2021, Appendix 1, Tables US-11 and US-24). The exact percentage changes depending on acreage threshold, region, question wording, and how one defines professional assistance, but the general pattern is clear enough: a lot of forestland is moving forward without much formal planning backbone attached to it.

That does not surprise me. Coming up with a plan is difficult. When you are managing ten acres at a time, what is the real benefit of creating a full formal management plan? You are not creating a plan for a landscape mosaic. You are creating a plan for your own backyard. And when you are operating at ten acres of scale, all sorts of things can impact that acreage quickly. Invasive species can spread across ten acres in a matter of years. Deer pressure can shape regeneration. Neighbor effects can matter a lot, because every boundary is close. A storm, a pest, a new owner next door, or a change in access can shift the practical reality of the stand.

From an academic sense, it does not make sense for everyone to have a plan everywhere all the time. It is impossible to manage that much space with the current shape of our forest industry. There are not enough foresters, not enough markets, not enough hours, and often not enough economic incentive to make every ten-acre ownership operate like a miniature public forest with a complete written apparatus around it.

But even when a formal plan is absent, the stand still has inertia.

That word keeps coming back to me. Inertia is the direction the system is already moving. In a northern hardwood stand, inertia might be high-grading legacy, too much deer pressure, invasive plants slowly taking more of an impact on the landscape, a lack of sufficient regeneration, a poor diameter structure, or a stand that is overstocked and waiting for some disturbance to decide its future. It might also be a healthy understory, decent regeneration, good vertical structure, and a canopy that is aging into something locally valuable.

That is really at the heart of the Northern Hardwoods Resilience Study. We took data from Forest Inventory and Analysis plots and many different state and local inventorying efforts on northern hardwood forests, and we tried to summarize what is happening for the system inertia of different northern hardwood stands. Are we seeing valuable tree species regenerating in sufficient capacities? Are we lacking sufficient regeneration? Across much of the range, we are seeing stands where sufficient regeneration of desirable or merchantable tree species is not where we would want it to be.

So when we talk about no plan being technically still a plan, that is not a clever little phrase floating above the woods. It is directly connected to forest outcomes. A stand can be left alone into resilience, or it can be left alone into decline. The difference depends on what is already happening in that stand and whether anyone is paying enough attention to notice.

New Jersey gave me a useful case study for thinking about this, though I do not want to single the state out as if it were the whole story. It is more like a visible example of a pattern that shows up in transition zones. I was passing by Red Barn Pond in New Jersey when I started thinking through this. In the northwest corner of the state, northern hardwood forests are present in large contiguous chunks: Stokes State Forest, High Point State Park, the Delaware Water Gap National Recreation Area, and other connected patches that make up a broad hardwood landscape.

But northern hardwoods do not occupy the center of forestry energy in New Jersey. The Pine Barrens take up so much of the state forestry scene. That means the northwest hardwood section can be passed up or receive far less tactical attention. There may be a management plan somewhere at the state level, but tactically, not much may happen in those hardwood stands. Markets may be limited. Bureaucratic frameworks can be steep. The public may be watching closely. Old growth characteristics are valued by many people, and many people do not like cuts when entries are made.

In that kind of situation, doing very little becomes easier than naming a specific hardwood strategy.

That same general pattern can show up in other transition zones too. If you are in Minnesota and much of the energy is going toward pine systems, or in New Jersey and much of the energy is going toward the Pine Barrens, northern hardwoods can become the forest type that is present but not always central. Attention is divided. Management capacity is divided. Markets are divided. The forest does not stop changing just because the management attention is elsewhere.

The fair version of this argument has to admit that doing very little can be legitimate. It can make sense when the acreage is too small to justify a rigid plan. It can make sense when hardwood markets are weak or far away. It can make sense when the local public values older forest structure, recreation, education, aesthetics, or the feeling of a forest that has not been harvested for 150 years. It can make sense when we do not know enough about the system yet, or when managers are waiting for the right amount of research before making larger decisions.

You could describe that as punting, literally. Sometimes punting is irresponsible. Sometimes it is the best available play.

As I walked through New Jersey, I saw stands that seemed to have relatively okay regeneration in the understory and great vertical structural diversity. If that is what the local managers and the local public are looking for, then maybe the inertia is acceptable in the near term. If those stands retain old-growth characteristics and provide recreation, education, and public value, that is not nothing. Leaving them alone may be an intentional choice with consequences that many people actually prefer.

The problem comes when nobody names the choice.

A lack of planning can look like neutrality from the outside, but forests do not operate inside a vacuum of planning. There are always factors at play that affect what you will or will not do. Markets are a factor. Public opinion is a factor. Deer are a factor. Invasive plants are a factor. Professional capacity is a factor. Ownership size is a factor. Bureaucracy is a factor. The fact that a landowner has not talked with a consulting forester is a factor. The fact that an agency has more urgent work in another forest type is a factor.

All of those things shape the future of the stand whether anyone writes them down or not.

That is why I think landowners and managers should at least try to know the inertia before deciding that no plan is fine. A consulting forester can help with that. If a landowner has long-term goals in mind, especially if they plan on passing land down between generations, a formalized plan can enable that landowner to actualize what a healthy stand could look like. A good, reputable consulting forester in the local area will have the expertise to know if something is actually wrong in the forest.

There is no certain formula for how northern hardwoods should look across their entire range. I already wrote in [NHR Log 10: There Shall Be No Universal Manual for Northern Hardwood Governance](https://www.mtntheman.com/northern-hardwoods/research/travel/2026/07/15/nhr-log-10-there-shall-be-no-universal-manual-for-northern-hardwood-governance.html) that there shall be no universal manual for northern hardwood governance, and the same idea applies silviculturally. A healthy northern hardwood stand in New Jersey may not look exactly like one in Maine, Michigan, Wisconsin, Ontario, or Vermont. Local foresters who are good at their craft will be able to tell you if a stand is unhealthy if you can ask the right questions.

Maybe that is the practical nudge here. Ask the right questions. What is regenerating? What is missing? Are the species we want in the next cohort actually present? Is deer pressure shaping the future more than we want to admit? Are invasives just beginning to take hold, or have they already become a major part of the understory? Is the stand overstocked? Is the current condition moving toward a future the landowner actually wants? If nothing changes, what is this forest likely to become?

That last question is the quiet heart of the whole post.

If nothing changes, what is this forest likely to become?

A plan does not have to be fifty pages to be meaningful. [NHR Log 12: Good Forestry Without 50 Pages](https://www.mtntheman.com/northern-hardwoods/research/travel/2026/07/20/nhr-log-12-good-forestry-without-50-pages.html) was partly about that idea: good forestry can happen without every thought being written in a giant formal document. But there is a difference between a thin plan, an informal plan, and no thought at all. A plan can be a written document, a conversation with a forester, a monitoring habit, a tax program requirement, a family succession conversation, or a clear decision to leave a forest alone because the stand is healthy enough and the owner values what it is becoming.

Leaving a forest alone is an acceptable outcome when you are aware of the inertia already there. It becomes more dangerous when it is only the default outcome of confusion, avoidance, lack of access, weak markets, or paralysis.

And I do wonder if northern hardwood managers across the board feel a sense of paralysis at all sorts of different levels. The system is naturally complex. The ownership mosaic is complex. The markets are uneven. The public values are complicated. The ecology is variable. The correct treatment is rarely universal. A person can look at all of that and decide, consciously or unconsciously, that it is easier to wait.

Sometimes waiting is wise. Sometimes waiting is how a stand keeps sliding.

No plan is technically still a plan because the forest will keep moving through time. That does not mean every acre needs a rigid management document. It does not mean every unmanaged stand is failing. It does not mean every agency or landowner should be blamed for doing very little. It means the absence of a plan should be treated as a choice with consequences, and the fairness of that choice depends on the forest itself.

If the inertia is good, maybe the plan is to protect it.

If the inertia is bad, maybe the plan needs to stop pretending it is absent.
