Good forestry on the ground takes time. You can write a plan all day, but nothing will get done in the woods.

That sounds obvious, maybe even too obvious, but I think it gets at one of the strange tensions in forestry. A lot of us are trained to think that a good forest management plan ought to be written down in tremendous detail. Every stand condition, every treatment objective, every ecological premise, every marking rule, every contingency. In a perfect world, maybe that would be true. We would write down exactly what we planned to do, why we planned to do it, and how each cut fit into some grander narrative of continuous forestry.

In actuality, people skip steps in that perfect model.

That does not automatically mean they are doing bad forestry.

I want to be careful here, because I am not trying to give poor documentation a pass. Documentation can hold foresters accountable. It can make intent visible. It can give the next person a way to understand what happened and why. Without proper planning, it is reasonable to expect that some outcomes will be worse than they otherwise could have been.

But it would be too simple to say that the problem is always the person with thin paperwork.

One of the ideas I keep coming back to from the span-of-control posts is that there are not enough hours in the day to get everything done that a forester needs to get done. I worked through that time-budget problem in [NHR Log 4: Span of Control in Forestry](https://www.mtntheman.com/northern-hardwoods/research/travel/2026/05/24/span-of-control-in-forestry.html), and then tried to make the tradeoffs more adjustable in [NHR Log 4.5: Updated Span of Control Calculator](https://www.mtntheman.com/northern-hardwoods/research/travel/2026/05/31/span-of-control-calculator-v2.html). Some managers operate in systems with very intensive reporting requirements. Others work in private-land contexts where the formal paperwork burden is much lighter. That difference is real. It shapes what gets written down, who checks it, and how much of the work becomes visible afterward.

The question, then, is whether good forestry can still happen when the documentation is sparse.

I think the answer is yes, but with caveats hanging all over it.

A good marking plan should usually incorporate good silvicultural practice, but good silvicultural practice does not always arrive attached to a complete marking plan. Sometimes the plan is in the paint. Sometimes it is in the forester walking tree by tree, deciding what stays, what goes, what the next cohort might look like, and what kind of residual canopy they are trying to leave behind.

I saw an example of this with a forester working primarily on private lands in a procurement capacity. There was no large written apparatus around the work. There was no fifty-page document explaining how they were going to mark responsibly. But they were still doing a full marking operation. They were marking trees to a spec, thinking through merchantable trees, ugly trees, residual canopy, and what would remain for the next entry.

Nobody had written, in formal language, "we will be leaving a responsible cohort." But that was ostensibly what they were doing.

That is a different kind of evidence. It is less legible from a paperwork standpoint, but it is not meaningless. There is judgment behind the paint. There is a difference between turning an operator loose with a vague cutting guide and sending someone into the stand with ecological concepts in their head and a spray gun in their hand.

That phrase, "spray paint gunner," can make the work sound simpler than it is. Marking trees is labor capacity in a busy system, yes. But it can also be one of the places where newer silvicultural thinking enters the woods. Young foresters come out of school with ecological principles, textbook arguments, field labs, and whatever early professional training they have picked up. Then they end up in the woods making thousands of small decisions.

Every forester starts out young. A lot of forestry knowledge still moves through apprenticeship. People talk to people. Foresters learn from supervisors, operators, landowners, professors, agency staff, consulting foresters, procurement foresters, and whoever else is in their local atmosphere. Those informal communication channels can be colorful, uneven, and highly dependent on who a person knows and how comfortable they are in a place.

That does not make them irrelevant.

A written plan is one way forestry knowledge moves through a system. It is a very important way. But knowledge also moves through seasonal work, tree-marking crews, conversations in trucks, arguments about acceptable growing stock and unacceptable growing stock, local habits, mistakes, second entries, and the old professional pattern of showing someone what to do until they can see it for themselves.

Some of that knowledge is technical expertise. Some of it is informal experience. Some of it is school. Some of it is the ability to look at a stand and say, without needing to write a paragraph first, that this tree should go and that one should stay.

The risk is that this argument can become too convenient. If we say that good forestry can happen without much documentation, someone can use that as an excuse to avoid writing down anything at all. That is not where I want this to land. Undocumented management is harder to evaluate. It is harder to audit. It is harder to explain to the next forester, the landowner, the public, or anyone trying to understand whether the work actually matched the intent.

Documentation is a form of responsibility.

But responsibility is also a form of responsibility.

There are people who write plans poorly and people who mark trees well. There are people with beautiful paperwork whose on-the-ground results are unimpressive, and there are people with sparse paperwork whose field judgment is much better than the file suggests. The paperwork matters, but it is not the whole forest.

Maybe that is the part that feels most important to me. Forestry for foresters can be difficult for many different reasons, but lack of documentation should probably be one of our first priorities for understanding why it gets difficult. It is difficult because plans take time. It is difficult because fieldwork takes time. It is difficult because there are usually more acres, more sales, more landowners, more conversations, and more follow-up obligations than one person can fully hold in their head.

So yes, write more down when you can. Make the intent visible. Leave a record for the next person. Treat documentation as part of the work rather than an annoying academic exercise after the real work is done.

But also, do not confuse the absence of a perfect written plan with the absence of forestry.

Sometimes good forestry is a document. Sometimes it is a conversation. Sometimes it is an apprenticeship. Sometimes it is a young forester bringing fresh ecological thinking into a private-land sale. Sometimes it is a line of paint on a tree, carrying more judgment than the file ever managed to say.
