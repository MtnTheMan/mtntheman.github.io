Where does wood go when markets are not there?

That was the question I kept circling back to after spending time in New York and seeing slash walls show up as part of the forestry conversation there. The idea had started in New York and then kind of extended up into New England, but you do not really see that same thing in the Lake States, at least not in the same way. Canada has tried it here and there too, from what I understand, but the geography of the practice tells part of the story.

Slash walls are interesting to me less because of how they look, though they are definitely hard to miss, and more because of the logic behind them. You have tree branches left behind. You have tops and slash material that come out of the woods after a harvest. You have wood that would otherwise have a low-value or no-value destination. Instead of pretending that material does not exist, a slash wall asks whether some utility can still be pulled out of it.

That is where the larger question begins. What happens with our low-value wood products? If they are not sent to firewood, if they are not sent to a mill, if they are not being utilized for something like a slash wall, what can you do with them? Does the material just lay there and stay? Many times, it probably does. It probably very much does lay there and stay.

When I say low-value wood, I am talking about a whole category of material that can look different depending on the stand, the harvest, the markets, and the region. It could be the tops of trees. It could be small-diameter wood. It could be wood that is under a certain size threshold, or wood from species that simply do not carry much value in a given market. It could be what people sometimes call "uggo wood," as in ugly wood, the stuff with imperfections, defects, poor form, or whatever other issue keeps it from becoming a higher-value product.

Low-value wood is an economic label, but it is also an operational headache and, in degraded woods especially, a pretty common outcome of history. In woods that have had a lot of human impact over the years, what we are often left with is a lot of low-value product. That does not mean the material is meaningless. It means the available market may not know what to do with it, may not want it, may only want it within a tight radius, or may only accept it under quotas.

Historically, parts of the Northeast and the broader northern hardwood belt had stronger markets for low-value products. Paper markets, pulp markets, and other wood fiber outlets could help absorb material that would never become sawlogs. Those markets have faded in many places. Maine is only one piece of the northern hardwood world, but a recent forecasting paper put one version of that change pretty bluntly: from 2000 to 2017, 64 percent of Maine's pulp and paper processing mills shut down (Crawley et al. 2025). Where pulp markets still exist, they are often heavily localized. A mill may only take material from so far away. A buyer may set limits on how much they will accept. A market can technically exist and still be unavailable to a particular job.

That is the conundrum. Low-value wood may still have value, but if the channel to move it is gone, the value becomes hard to realize. When markets cannot accept that good, what is left to really work with there?

The no-action choice is always sitting in the background. Leave it there. Let the tops, branches, and residual material stay on site. Sometimes that is a valid ecological choice. Woody debris can have value in the forest. It can protect soils, cycle nutrients, provide structure, and contribute to the messiness that forests are actually made of. But leaving material behind can also be a failure of markets. It can be money left on the table, and for many forest managers, losing a pulp market or another low-grade outlet can thin the margins that make jobs feasible in the first place.

That is where the uncomfortable mix begins. When markets fail, it gets harder to do the right ecological thing, and then logistically it becomes more difficult too. A forester might look at a pile of low-value material and think about a slash wall, but somebody has to build it. Somebody has to know how to build it. If the operator has never made one before, then there is a learning curve, and that learning curve happens in the woods, on a real site, with real equipment. They might spend time figuring it out. They might tear the earth up a little bit trying to learn how to make a slash wall for the first time. That is always going to be a difficult balance as a forester.

The appeal of slash walls, at least as I understand them, is tied partly to deer and regeneration. Deer pressure can shape the vegetation dynamics of northern hardwood stands in very real ways, and the old problem of getting the next cohort through the browse layer does not go away just because the tops and branches are inconvenient (Horsley et al. 2003). If slash can become a rough browse barrier, then maybe low-value material becomes part of a regeneration strategy rather than a leftover problem. That logic is compelling. It is also easy to admire from a distance, before the costs, equipment time, layout decisions, operator experience, and site conditions start making the idea less clean.

Low-value material does not become free to use simply because it is already lying there. Transforming low-value wood into something useful often still costs money. It may cost money to sort it, move it, chip it, pile it, transport it, process it, or arrange it into a structure that has some ecological function. A slash wall can turn unwanted tops and branches into a deer browse barrier that may improve stand quality later on, especially where regeneration pressure is intense. But building one is still an operation. It requires time, skill, planning, and a reason to believe the benefit is worth the cost.

The same basic question follows other possible destinations too. Firewood is one outlet, but it depends on species, accessibility, labor, moisture, equipment, and a buyer. Pulp can be a good outlet where pulp markets still exist, but the radius matters and quotas matter. Biomass can absorb some low-grade fiber, but it has its own economics and politics. Biochar is one of the more interesting emerging possibilities, with a groundswell of support around it, though it still has to become a market that can reliably move material at the scale forests produce it. Biochar can be produced from biomass through oxygen-limited heating, and its climate and soil value depends heavily on feedstock, process, end use, and whether the economics actually work (Woolf et al. 2010).

Mass timber is another future-facing possibility, especially if hardwoods can eventually become a larger part of that product stream, although that is a different kind of material pathway than finding somewhere for residual slash to go. More broadly, the USDA Forest Products Laboratory has framed underutilized woody biomass as exactly this kind of utilization problem: small-diameter and thinning material can be costly to remove, and commercial uses can improve the economics of forest health work if those uses actually exist on the ground (USDA Forest Products Laboratory n.d.). That last part is doing a lot of work. A possible product is not the same thing as a functioning market. A promising technology is not the same thing as a buyer within hauling distance.

The common thread is that responsible disposal is becoming part of forest management. It is not enough to say that low-value wood exists and someone should use it. The question is who can use it, where, at what cost, under what rules, and with what ecological outcome.

That is where governance starts to enter the picture for me. If low-value markets continue to fade, then the choices left to foresters will depend on either new channels for moving low-value wood fiber or better incentives for doing responsible things with it where market value is weak. That could mean incentives for slash walls in places where deer pressure makes them useful. It could mean investment in biochar infrastructure. It could mean finding new ways to use small-diameter or low-grade hardwood fiber. It could mean supporting markets that are local enough to actually function for the people doing the work on the ground.

Rebuilding old markets seems like a tough sell. I do not think the future is simply going back to a time when pulp markets could absorb everything that needed somewhere to go. The better question may be how many new partial answers we can create. Some material may go to firewood. Some may go to pulp where pulp still works. Some may stay in the woods for good ecological reasons. Some may become slash walls. Some may become biochar. Some may end up in products we have not really figured out yet.

There might even be new solutions we have not thought of yet.

For now, the question keeps following me around the northern hardwood belt: where does wood go when markets are not there? It sounds like a simple utilization question, but it opens into something bigger. It is about degraded stands and thin margins. It is about operators and learning curves. It is about whether low-value wood is treated as waste, habitat, fuel, fiber, structure, or some future product waiting on the right incentive. And it is about whether responsible forestry can still make financial and logistical sense when one of the basic outlets for low-grade material disappears.
