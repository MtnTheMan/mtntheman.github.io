I started thinking through this post while I was "marooned side on the road," locked out of my car, waiting for help, and trying to make something useful out of the situation. That is probably fitting, because the idea itself was about what happens when people are stuck.

Foresters can get stuck in all kinds of ways. They can operate in a vacuum of support, or a vacuum of markets, or a vacuum of whatever they need to actually get the work done. They may know that a management action needs to happen, but they do not physically possess the saws required to do that work. They do not possess the manpower required to do that work. They may lack the money, the personnel, the operator skill set, the public support, or the nearby market that would make the job feasible.

So who fills the gaps in that scenario?

A lot of that comes back to partnerships.

The importance of partnerships is a theme that has come up before on this trip. There are places where you have a lot of independent actors. You can have very large, successful businesses that do a lot of things as their own entity. A large timber company can support loggers, support foresters, build roads, hire truckers, operate a mill, and manage a whole portfolio under one combined mission. Some forestry businesses are extremely vertically integrated, and when they are operating well, they can pursue their economic objectives while also trying to manage for ecologically sound silviculture.

But a lot of forestry does not happen inside that kind of structure.

There are many other businesses and organizations that operate on extremely small margins. Individual foresters, independent consultants, county governments, municipalities, land trusts, conservation organizations, small agencies, and local people with local responsibilities may all be trying to do good work in the woods. They may care deeply about the forest. They may have a clear sense that something important needs to happen. And still, they may not be able to do it alone.

That is where partnerships become essential. Partnerships between firms can help accomplish needs. Partnerships between agencies that represent similar interests can help move work from an idea into an action. A procurement forester may rely on loggers to get the work done, but may also rely on state grants to kind of pursue a positive forestry mission in the woods. A state with a ton of acres to manage may rely on good consulting foresters and good loggers to carry out the work. A conservation organization may have a cultural or ecological objective, but need money, operators, permitting knowledge, and local trust to make that objective real.

In the end, the hope is that you have a large holistic mission that accomplishes the different tenets of good forestry. A vertically integrated company may be able to hold many of those pieces under one roof. In other places, it is the partnerships that enable something to work like that.

Southern New England offers a good case study in what forestry looks like when it is struggling, when it is treading a lot of water. In Massachusetts, Connecticut, and Rhode Island, there are foresters who are really striving to do good work, and they are not very well noticed. They are occupying a space that is densely populated. It is a tough place to do forestry, and a tough crowd to please.

I have already written about some of that tension in [NHR Log 6: Forestry as the Centrist](https://www.mtntheman.com/northern-hardwoods/research/travel/2026/07/06/nhr-log-6-forestry-as-the-centrist.html) and [NHR Log 9: Do We All Remember the Lorax Properly?](https://www.mtntheman.com/northern-hardwoods/research/travel/2026/07/13/nhr-log-9-do-we-all-remember-the-lorax-properly.html). When you are working in those spaces, you have to deal with public perception in a way that still achieves the common good of forestry. You are trying to satisfy market demands and ecological demands, but you are doing so with a laser focus on whatever you are doing from the political lens. And often, you are doing it with a lack of support.

Markets tend to be far away in southern New England. Parcels can be small and fragmented. The forest itself may be surrounded by people who have very strong feelings about what should happen there, and many of those feelings are reasonable, even when they make the practical work more complicated. That makes southern New England a really good vacuum for thinking about partnerships. It shows what happens when forestry cannot assume that the old systems will be there to catch it.

One example that has stuck with me is eastern white cedar.

Eastern white cedar is a sensitive species that occupies wetlands. It is not exclusively a northern hardwood species, but I was lucky enough to go on a nature walk near Monson, Massachusetts, where we talked about the Indigenous importance of eastern white cedar and how it has been diminished on the landscape. To bring back eastern white cedar, or to strengthen its resiliency in the limited habitat areas where it is still extant, you basically need to clear out everything else and allow for regeneration of the eastern white cedar to thrive.

That sounds simple until you start asking who can actually do it.

You have a limited amount of acreage. You are operating in a wetland space, which means you need to be really sensitive. You may not have a ton of loggers who can actually do the work. You may have a skill set gap, a personnel gap, and a funding gap all at once. Indigenous communities may have a cultural interest in the species. A group like Mass Audubon may have conservation capacity or land relationships. Local governments may be able to front money or help fund an operation. Operators may know what is physically possible. Foresters may know what the stand needs.

None of those pieces are the whole thing by themselves.

The partnership is what starts to make the work possible.

That is why I initially wanted to call this a democratic form of forestry, though I think diplomatic is probably the better word. Different groups inherently have different interests, different conflicts, and different ways of looking at the landscape. When they engage in a partnership, they are trying to find mutualism to achieve a common goal.

That mutualism can change the forestry itself. You end up with a middle ground. In the eastern white cedar example, the goal is not a pure conservation mandate in the abstract. It is also not a purely economic operation. There is a cultural component, a cultural aspect that might be lost on the landscape, or would be lost if the trajectory of landscape degradation continues in its current way. The partnership can produce a better definition of the common good, because the work starts to reflect what the group of partners agrees on.

It is like a band, like a rock band. Every artist, every member of a rock band, when you are writing a song, will bring what they have to the table. They contribute to their best ability in the studio, and hopefully the composition turns out to be a really great song. Does it always end up being a great song? Definitely not.

That is worth remembering in forestry too. If the group of partners is missing a perspective, the final product will probably show that absence. If no one at the table understands what an operator might need, want, or be able to make money from, then the plan may lack the economic feasibility needed to get the work done in the woods. If a conservation group is concerned with being so careful around resources that they are never aggressive enough to make positive changes, the work can stall in another way. Sometimes you need to be more aggressive, and that is just the truth of forestry.

Appropriately aggressive forestry is a gray area. There is no universal amount of aggression that will be correct across all sites. It would be impossible to advise a young forester that they will achieve the correct amount of aggressive action on the first job of their career. It is entirely place dependent.

In a sensitive place like a marsh or a bog, you really do want to be careful. If you are talking about 20,000 acres of upland yellow birch habitat, you can probably take away more. A general trend in the interviews has been that foresters may be able to remove more basal area than we had previously thought. Some interviewees have even suggested that we can afford to be a little bit more aggressive in what we would call sugar maple stands. Does the data bear that out? We will wait and see.

The larger point is that the right action depends on stand conditions and what is most ecologically prudent for that time and that place. A partnership can give confidence by lending in a lot of perspectives to how something gets done.

There is also a real limit here. You can have too many cooks in the kitchen. You can have too many voices and too much planning, to the point where nothing gets done or people are no longer effectively using their time. Many foresters already lack the time to do full-fledged plans because they are too busy actually out there in the woods trying to do the work.

But I do not want the burden of partnership to become the focus. Anyone entering a partnership expects to spend more time working with partners and keeping things diplomatic. The bigger story is that partnerships can enable forestry to be successful when it might not otherwise be feasible in the space where a forester is operating.

There is something a little bit heroic about that.

The heroism is persistence to try to do the right thing. It is diplomacy to work with your friends and peers. It is seriousness in trying to achieve what is right for the ecology while also serving human interests and needs in that space. That is an essential utilitarian point behind forestry as a whole. Forest management is not a field built around pretending humans have no needs. It is a field built around asking how human needs and forest needs can be held together with diligence, wisdom, and care.

Can wisdom be heroic?

I think it can be, especially when the easier story is rugged individualism. In the U.S., there is a strong attachment to that idea. The heroic figure is often imagined as the lone actor, the person who does everything independently, the person who does not need help. But rugged individualism can only take you so far.

Forestry in the 21st century is showing the limits of that mentality. Markets are disappearing across the board. It gets harder and harder to do forestry in a 20th century, maybe even late 19th century model, when those markets disappear. Large vertically integrated forestry operations may be fine because they work on economies of scale. But northern hardwood forests do not exist only as large, easy-to-manage blocks. Across the range, there are so many small, fragmented parcels that add up to this large landscape mosaic, and that mosaic is difficult to manage or even advise upon.

In order to make that mosaic more cohesive across the board, we are going to have to rely on partnerships.

I do not think climate change makes it easier to own and manage hardwoods responsibly. I do not think disappearing markets make it easier either. The challenges are getting larger, and many of the people trying to respond to those challenges are working with limited time, limited money, limited staff, and limited public patience.

So, in the interest of resiliency, we look forward to partnerships to really carry us forward into the 21st century.

That does not make partnerships perfect. It makes them necessary. And maybe, if they are done well, it makes them something more than necessary. It makes them a diplomatic form of forestry, where people bring what they have to the table, search for mutualism, and try to write a better composition than any one of them could have written alone.
